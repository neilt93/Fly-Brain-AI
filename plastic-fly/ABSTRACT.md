# Emergent Sensorimotor Behaviors from the Drosophila Connectome Without Learning

The Drosophila melanogaster FlyWire connectome provides a complete wiring diagram of 139,000 neurons and 15 million synapses, but whether this structure alone — without any parameter tuning or learning — is sufficient to produce adaptive behavior remains unknown. We built a closed-loop sensorimotor system that couples a Brian2 leaky integrate-and-fire simulation of the full FlyWire connectome to a MuJoCo-based biomechanical fly body, with biologically identified sensory neuron populations encoding body state and environmental stimuli into the brain, and descending neuron populations decoding motor commands back to the body. Using this system, we demonstrate three connectome-encoded behaviors that emerge from wiring alone: causal locomotion control (10/10 ablation tests, forward drive reduced 53% by targeted silencing), olfactory valence discrimination (opposite turning for attractive vs aversive odors, 6/6 tests), and visually guided looming escape (contralateral turning with escape index 1.11, abolished 21-fold in shuffled connectome controls). These results establish that the connectome's synaptic wiring diagram, with uniform biophysical parameters and no learning, is sufficient to produce directional, stimulus-specific sensorimotor transformations — a finding that constrains theories of neural development and provides a computational baseline against which the contributions of plasticity, neuromodulation, and experience can be measured.
